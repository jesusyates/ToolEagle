If you run one domain for global and localized content, you need both engines to *understand* your site—even when traffic skews toward one market.

## Same fundamentals

Google and Baidu both need:

- **Stable HTTP responses** (200 for public content).
- **Clear site structure** (internal links, sitemaps).
- **Unique titles and meta descriptions** per URL.
- **Language signals** that match the audience (hreflang, URL patterns, or separate sections like `/zh`).

## Where they diverge

1. **Market share by region** — Global English queries lean Google; mainland Chinese queries lean Baidu. Neither replaces the other for cross-border creators.
2. **Console tooling** — Google Search Console and Baidu Webmaster Tools are separate; you verify and submit sitemaps in each.
3. **Latency and hosting** — Overseas hosting can be crawled by both, but **speed and availability** to users in mainland China affect engagement metrics, which indirectly affect SEO.
4. **Content expectations** — Thin translated pages underperform everywhere; native context wins for local intent.

## What to do on one site

- Keep **one canonical URL** per piece of content; avoid duplicate paths with the same text.
- Segment **English vs Chinese** clearly (paths or subdomains—your stack already supports `/zh`).
- Publish **substantive** articles on schedule; daily publishing is fine if each page adds new information—see our article on *volume without spam signals* in this section.

<TipBox title="Reality check">ICP filing and mainland hosting are **legal and product** decisions for China-facing services—not a guaranteed “ranking switch” by itself.</TipBox>
