Search engines are not magic—they run a pipeline. When you know the three stages, you stop guessing why a URL is missing from results.

## Crawl: can bots reach the URL?

**Crawling** means an automated program (often called a *bot* or *spider*) requests your URL over HTTP, like a browser without a human.

For creators and tool sites, common blockers include:

1. **robots.txt** — A file at `/robots.txt` can disallow paths. If you block `/blog/` or your whole site by mistake, crawlers may skip you.
2. **Authentication** — Pages behind login are usually not indexed for general search.
3. **Broken links** — If nothing links to a page and it is not in a sitemap, discovery is slower.
4. **Server errors** — Frequent 5xx responses train crawlers to deprioritize your host.

<TipBox title="Quick check">Open your page in an incognito window with JavaScript disabled (or use a fetch tool). If the main text never appears, search engines may see thin or empty HTML—fix rendering or SSR first.</TipBox>

## Index: is the page in the catalog?

**Indexing** means the engine stores your URL in its *index* (a giant catalog) so it can be returned for a query.

A page can be crawled but not indexed if it is duplicate, low-value, blocked by a `noindex` directive, or filtered as spam-like. Unique titles, clear headings, and real answers to a search intent help.

## Rank: who wins the query?

**Ranking** decides order among indexed pages. Signals include relevance, quality, freshness, backlinks, and—for many queries—how well the page satisfies the user in one session.

**Bottom line:** Fix **crawl** first (reachable, not disallowed), then **index** (unique, indexable), then invest in **rank** (depth, trust, and intent match).
