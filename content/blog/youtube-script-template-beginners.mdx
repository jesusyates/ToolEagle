## YouTube Script Template for Beginners

1. Start your script with a question that targets your viewer's immediate problem.
2. Promise a clear benefit within the first five seconds to stop the scroll.
3. Use a quick, relatable story to establish an emotional connection early.
4. State the video's single, most valuable takeaway right after the hook.
5. Preview the three main steps or points you will cover in detail.
6. Your first main point should address the most common beginner mistake.
7. Explain that point with a specific, real-world example from your niche.
8. Transition using a phrase like 'Now that you understand X, let's tackle Y.'
9. Your second point should introduce a simple, actionable tool or method.
10. Show a brief screen recording of you using that tool for clarity.
11. Mention a potential pitfall here to build credibility and trust.
12. The third point should focus on the implementation of the first two.
13. Give viewers a very small, immediate task they can complete now.
14. Use a lower-third graphic to reinforce a key term or statistic.
15. Pose a rhetorical question to the viewer to keep them engaged.
16. Include a quick, relevant B-roll shot to visually break up the monologue.
17. Signal the upcoming conclusion with a phrase like 'To wrap this up...'
18. Succinctly re-state the core lesson from the beginning of the video.
19. Ask viewers to comment with which tip they will try first.
20. Direct them to a related playlist for deeper learning on this topic.
21. Prompt a like if they found the step-by-step breakdown helpful.
22. Clearly tell them to subscribe and hit the notification bell icon.
23. Mention your next video's topic to create anticipation for return viewers.
24. End with a final, encouraging statement about their creator journey.
25. For beauty tutorials, the hook could address a specific makeup frustration.
26. In tech reviews, hook with a definitive verdict on a product's value.
27. Gaming channels can hook with a shocking clip of a game moment.
28. The main points for a DIY video are: plan, gather, assemble, finish.
29. A cooking video's beats are: introduce dish, show ingredients, demonstrate steps, present result.
30. For a vlog, structure around: setting the scene, the main event, reflection, looking ahead.
31. Fitness videos often follow: present exercise, demonstrate form, state reps/sets, give modification.
32. A 'storytime' video needs: context setup, conflict introduction, climax moment, lesson learned.
33. Use active voice like 'You will learn' instead of passive 'This will be shown.'
34. Write spoken contractions like 'you're' and 'don't' to sound natural, not formal.
35. Read your script aloud to catch awkward phrasing before recording.
36. Time your script; aim for 60-90 seconds per 100 words as a rough guide.
37. Place visual cues in brackets, like [SHOW SCREENSHOT OF DASHBOARD] for editing.
38. Sound effect cues, like [SFX: DING], help editors add auditory emphasis.
39. Vary your sentence length between short, punchy statements and longer explanations.
40. Insert deliberate pauses by writing '...' for moments of emphasis or transition.
41. Define one niche-specific acronym or term if absolutely necessary for clarity.
42. Avoid jargon that your ideal beginner viewer would not already know.
43. The CTA must be a single, unambiguous action, not a list of options.
44. Place your primary CTA both verbally and as an on-screen end screen.
45. Your video title should mirror the core promise made in the hook.
46. Create a thumbnail that visually represents the outcome of your tutorial.
47. YouTube's algorithm favors videos that retain audience attention past the 30-second mark.
48. A strong hook directly impacts your click-through rate from search and suggested videos.
49. Clear scripting reduces rambling, which improves your audience retention metrics.
50. This structure provides a repeatable framework, freeing mental energy for content ideas.
